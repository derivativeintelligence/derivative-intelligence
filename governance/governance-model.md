# Governance Model: Derivative Intelligence

---

Governance is the foundation of trust in intelligence systems.

Without it, systems drift toward opacity, centralization, and misalignment.

Derivative Intelligence (DI) proposes a new model:

**Principle-aligned, transparent, and verifiable governance.**

---

## Why Governance Matters

Modern intelligence systems are governed by:

- internal policies  
- centralized decision-making  
- non-transparent updates  

This results in:

- inconsistent behavior  
- limited accountability  
- erosion of trust  

As these systems increasingly shape decisions, knowledge access, and real-world outcomes, governance must evolve from implicit control to **explicit, auditable systems**.

---

## Governance Objectives

The DI governance model is designed to ensure:

- alignment with a stable corpus of guiding principles  
- transparency in all decisions and updates  
- verifiability of system behavior and changes  
- resistance to centralization and unilateral control  
- long-term mission integrity  

---

## Structural Model

The DI ecosystem is structured to balance **mission integrity** with **practical execution**.

---

### 1. Derivative Intelligence Organization (Non-Profit)

Responsible for:

- defining and maintaining the foundational corpus  
- stewarding the philosophical and ethical framework  
- overseeing governance processes  
- protecting long-term alignment  

This entity does not operate commercial systems.

It acts as the **constitutional steward**.

---

### 2. Foundation-Controlled Structure

A foundation entity maintains **controlling interest (≥51%)** in any for-profit operating entities.

Purpose:

- prevent mission drift  
- enforce adherence to guiding principles  
- ensure governance continuity  

---

### 3. For-Profit Operating Entities

Responsible for:

- building DI-powered systems  
- deploying products and infrastructure  
- scaling adoption  
- raising capital  

Constraints:

- must align with the foundational corpus  
- must comply with governance processes  
- must remain transparent and auditable  

---

## Governance Layers

---

### 1. Foundational Corpus (Constitutional Layer)

A stable, explicit set of guiding principles.

Properties:

- transparent  
- version-controlled  
- difficult to change  
- globally accessible  

Changes:

- rare and deliberate  
- require formal governance process  
- require broad consensus  

This layer acts as the **constitution of the system**.

---

### 2. Interpretation Layer (Evolving Layer)

Provides context and application of principles.

Includes:

- scholarly interpretation  
- multi-perspective analysis  
- domain-specific adaptations  

Governed through:

- open proposals  
- peer review  
- versioned updates  

No single entity controls interpretation.

---

### 3. Alignment Layer (System Behavior)

Defines how systems behave in practice.

Includes:

- model constraints  
- policy frameworks  
- behavioral guardrails  

Requirements:

- explicit definition  
- documented changes  
- auditable implementation  

---

### 4. Knowledge Layer (Dynamic Input)

Continuously evolving data and information sources.

Includes:

- scientific knowledge  
- real-world data  
- system inputs  

This layer evolves rapidly but must remain **bounded by principles**.

---

### 5. Transparency & Verification Layer

Ensures all governance actions are:

- visible  
- traceable  
- verifiable  

This is where trust is enforced.

---

## On-Chain Governance & Immutability

To ensure long-term integrity, critical elements of governance are **anchored on-chain**.

---

### Purpose

On-chain anchoring provides:

- immutability of records  
- tamper-resistant history  
- globally accessible verification  

It establishes a **source of truth independent of any single entity**.

---

### What is Anchored

Where appropriate, the following are recorded or referenced on-chain:

- hashes of the foundational corpus  
- governance proposals and outcomes  
- interpretation updates  
- system-level changes  
- audit logs of critical decisions  

Full data may remain off-chain, while:

→ **on-chain records guarantee integrity**

---

### Immutability

On-chain records:

- cannot be altered  
- can only be superseded through new governance actions  
- remain permanently auditable  

This creates a **continuous, verifiable history of system evolution**.

---

### Design Approach

The system follows a hybrid architecture:

- off-chain for scalability and flexibility  
- on-chain for verification and trust  

Blockchain is not the system itself.

It is the **verification layer**.

---

## Proposal & Decision Process

All meaningful changes follow a structured governance flow.

---

### 1. Proposal

Anyone may submit proposals, including:

- principle updates  
- interpretations  
- system alignment changes  
- governance improvements  

---

### 2. Public Review

Proposals are:

- openly discussed  
- challenged  
- refined  

Transparency is required at all stages.

---

### 3. Evaluation

Evaluation criteria:

- alignment with foundational corpus  
- long-term system impact  
- coherence with existing framework  

---

### 4. Decision

Outcomes:

- approval  
- rejection  
- revision  

Decisions are:

- documented  
- justified  
- recorded  

---

### 5. Implementation

Approved changes are:

- versioned  
- logged  
- deployed transparently  

Where applicable, they are:

→ **anchored on-chain for verification**

---

## Participation Model

Governance is:

- community-driven  
- globally accessible  
- progressively merit-based  

Participants include:

- researchers  
- engineers  
- domain experts  
- contributors  

Over time, participation may evolve to include:

- reputation-based systems  
- contribution-weighted influence  
- decentralized governance mechanisms

## Safeguards

---

### Anti-Centralization

No single entity should control:

- interpretation  
- alignment decisions  
- system evolution  

---

### Mission Lock

The foundational corpus and governance structure:

- cannot be easily overridden  
- require high thresholds for change  

---

### Auditability

Every meaningful change must be:

- inspectable  
- explainable  
- verifiable  

---

### Verifiable Integrity

Critical actions must be:

- cryptographically anchored  
- independently verifiable  
- resistant to tampering  

---

## Risks & Considerations

---

### Governance Complexity

Distributed systems require:

- structured processes  
- clear coordination mechanisms  

---

### Interpretation Conflicts

Multiple perspectives must be:

- preserved  
- documented  
- reconciled constructively  

---

### Governance Capture

Mitigated through:

- transparency  
- broad participation  
- on-chain verification  

---

### Over-Reliance on Mechanisms

Governance tools must not replace:

- human judgment  
- principled reasoning  

---

## Long-Term Vision

We envision a future where:

- intelligence systems are governed like public infrastructure  
- alignment is grounded in principles, not policies  
- decisions are transparent, auditable, and verifiable  
- trust does not depend on institutions, but on systems  

---

## Final Statement

Without governance, intelligence systems drift.

With principled and verifiable governance, they align.
