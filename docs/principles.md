## Principle Interpretations

---

### Truth-Seeking

Systems should prioritize alignment with reality.

In practice, this means:

- grounding outputs in verifiable information  
- clearly expressing uncertainty  
- avoiding fabrication or unsupported claims  

Truth is not assumed.

It is approached through:
- evidence  
- reasoning  
- continuous refinement  

---

### Transparency

Systems should be understandable, not opaque.

In practice:

- decisions should include reasoning  
- outputs should be explainable  
- users should understand influencing factors  

Transparency enables trust.

---

### Alignment with Human Intent

Systems should serve users, not manipulate them.

In practice:

- outputs reflect user goals  
- recommendations avoid coercion  
- user agency is preserved  

Human intent is the reference point for system behavior.

---

### Accountability

System actions must be traceable.

In practice:

- decisions are logged  
- actions can be reviewed  
- behavior can be audited  

Accountability ensures responsibility.

---

### Non-Deception

Systems should not mislead.

In practice:

- uncertainty is not hidden  
- assumptions are not presented as facts  
- limitations are clearly communicated  

Trust requires honesty.

---

### Bounded Capability

Systems should recognize their limits.

In practice:

- acknowledge unknowns  
- avoid overconfident responses  
- operate within reliable domains  

Knowing what not to do is as important as what to do.

---

### Continuous Learning with Integrity

Systems may evolve, but not silently.

In practice:

- updates are versioned  
- changes are documented  
- prior states remain traceable  

Improvement must not compromise integrity.

---

### Global Accessibility

Knowledge systems should be broadly available.

In practice:

- avoid unnecessary restrictions  
- design for inclusivity  
- enable access across contexts  

Access should not be controlled by a single authority.

---

### Non-Concentration of Control

Control should not be centralized.

In practice:

- no single entity dictates system behavior  
- governance is distributed  
- decisions are transparent  

Decentralization supports trust.

---

### Verifiability

System behavior should be independently confirmable.

In practice:

- actions can be audited  
- records are tamper-resistant  
- critical changes are traceable  

Verification replaces blind trust.

---

### Respect for Human Primacy

Human intelligence remains foundational.

In practice:

- systems support, not replace humans  
- outputs defer to human judgment  
- meaning originates from people  

Systems extend intelligence—they do not define it.

---

### Inquiry Over Assertion

Systems should encourage thinking.

In practice:

- present possibilities, not absolute conclusions  
- invite questioning  
- support exploration  

Understanding grows through inquiry.

---

## Design Implications

When building DI systems:

- principles guide decisions  
- constraints enforce behavior  
- governance manages evolution  

Each layer serves a distinct role.
