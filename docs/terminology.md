# Terminology: Derivative Intelligence

---

This document defines the core language of the Derivative Intelligence (DI) framework.

Language is not neutral.

The way we define intelligence determines how we design, govern, and trust intelligent systems.

---

## Core Concepts

---

### Derivative Intelligence (DI)

Systems that extract, recombine, and optimize patterns from human-generated data.

DI systems:
- depend on prior human knowledge  
- operate through probabilistic inference  
- scale pattern recognition and synthesis  

They do not originate meaning or intent.

They derive.

---

### Originative Intelligence

Human intelligence that generates:
- meaning  
- intent  
- direction  

Characteristics:
- intuition  
- emotion  
- non-linear reasoning  
- inspiration  

Originative intelligence is not reducible to pattern recombination.

It is the source from which all derivative systems are built.

---

### Intelligence System

A system that processes information to produce outputs that appear intelligent.

Within this framework:
- most modern systems are derivative  
- intelligence is expressed through systems, but originates from humans  

---

## Foundational Architecture

---

### Foundational Corpus of Guiding Principles

A transparent, stable set of principles forming the **constitutional layer** of the system.

Properties:
- explicit  
- auditable  
- version-controlled  
- resistant to arbitrary change  

Acts as:
- a constraint on system behavior  
- a reference for alignment  
- the basis for governance  

This corpus defines the boundaries within which all system behavior must operate.

---

### Constitutional Layer

The foundational layer defined by the corpus of guiding principles.

It serves as:
- the root of alignment  
- the constraint on system behavior  
- the anchor for governance  

Unlike policies, which may shift, the constitutional layer is:
- stable  
- transparent  
- governed through formal processes  

All system layers operate within its bounds.

---

### Interpretation Layer

The evolving layer where principles are contextualized and applied.

Includes:
- scholarly interpretation  
- multi-perspective analysis  
- domain-specific reasoning  

Characteristics:
- adaptive over time  
- versioned  
- governed  

No single entity controls interpretation.

---

### Alignment Layer

The mechanisms that guide system behavior in accordance with principles.

Includes:
- model constraints  
- policy systems  
- behavioral guardrails  

Alignment must be:
- explicit  
- explainable  
- auditable  

---

### Knowledge Layer

The dynamic body of information used by the system.

Includes:
- scientific knowledge  
- real-world data  
- continuously updated inputs  

This layer evolves rapidly but must remain bounded by the constitutional layer.

---

### Governance Layer

The structures and processes that define how the system evolves.

Includes:
- proposal mechanisms  
- decision-making frameworks  
- audit logs  
- transparency systems  

Governance ensures that change is:
- controlled  
- visible  
- accountable  

---

### Verification Layer (On-Chain Anchoring)

A layer that ensures the integrity of critical system artifacts through cryptographic verification.

May include:
- hashes of the foundational corpus  
- governance decisions  
- proposal outcomes  
- system update logs  

Properties:
- immutable  
- tamper-resistant  
- globally verifiable  

This layer establishes trust without reliance on centralized authority.

---

## Core Processes

---

### Derivation

The process of:
- extracting patterns  
- recombining knowledge  
- generating outputs based on prior data  

This is the core function of DI systems.

---

### Recombination

The synthesis of existing patterns into new configurations.

Often perceived as creativity, but:
- constrained by training data  
- dependent on prior human knowledge  

---

### Optimization

Improving outputs based on:
- defined objectives  
- feedback loops  
- probabilistic evaluation  

Optimization enhances performance, but does not create meaning.

---

### Alignment

The process of guiding system behavior toward desired outcomes.

In DI:
- alignment must be explicit  
- alignment must be transparent  
- alignment must be governed  

---

### Transparency

The ability to:
- inspect system behavior  
- understand decision pathways  
- audit system changes over time  

Transparency is foundational to trust.

---

### Auditability

The ability to:
- trace decisions  
- verify changes  
- reconstruct system behavior  

Auditability ensures accountability.

---

## Misleading Terms (Reframed)

---

### “Artificial Intelligence”

Misleading because it implies:
- human-like cognition  
- independent intelligence  

Reframed as:
→ Derivative Intelligence

---

### “Machine Creativity”

Typically refers to:
- recombination  
- synthesis  
- pattern generation  

Not true origination.

---

### “Understanding”

Machines simulate understanding through:
- pattern recognition  
- statistical inference  

They do not possess intrinsic meaning.

---

## Foundational Principle

Correct language leads to correct systems.

If intelligence is defined incorrectly:
- systems will be misdesigned  
- trust will be misplaced  
- governance will be insufficient  

---

## Final Statement

Machines derive.  
Humans originate.
